PYP Exhibition Planner

AISA Grade 5 Inquiry Guide

1. Introductions & Group Dynamics

Mentor Introduction: Why is your mentor excited to work with you on this topic?

Take a moment to get to know each other before diving into the work!

Group Reflection: How will we use the Learner Profile traits of being Reflective and Open-Minded as a team?

As we start this journey, how will we listen to each other's ideas and reflect on our teamwork?

2. Tuning In & Building Knowledge

The Issue vs. The Goal: What is the specific problem, and what is the ultimate goal?

Remember: The issue is the problem (e.g., "Species Extinction") and the goal is what we want to achieve (e.g., "Life on Land"). Clear this up before you begin!

What initially drew your group to the topic of endangered animals?

When you hear the phrase "endangered animals," what is the very first image or thought that comes to mind?

What do you already know for a fact, and what might be a misconception?

List your facts and assumptions. Your mentor will help you understand complex ideas and spot any misconceptions (things you think are true, but might not be) early on!

Global Impact: Which Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) does this connect to?

Tip: Check out the UN's SDG Comics to help you understand the global impact of this goal in a fun, visual way!

3. Widening the Lens (Perspective)

If we were to look at this issue from the perspective of a conservationist working to protect a species, what might they say?

How might a local community that relies on hunting, fishing, or farming near an animal's habitat explain their point of view?

Who else is involved in this issue besides the animals and the conservationists?

Think about lawmakers, zoos, tourists, poachers, logging or mining companies, indigenous communities, etc.

4. Digging into the "How" and "Why" (Causation & Form)

What exactly causes animals to become endangered?

For example: habitat loss, poaching, pollution, climate change, invasive species.

What happens when a species goes extinct? How does it affect its ecosystem and the humans connected to it?

Are the laws that protect endangered animals the same everywhere in the world?

5. Data Collection & Field Research

How can we measure awareness about endangered animals in our school community?

Could you create a survey asking students/teachers to name endangered species, guess how many are left in the wild, or identify everyday habits that harm wildlife?

How will you use this data at your exhibition stand?

For example: "We will have iPads at our stand where visitors can take the survey, and we will display the live results on a screen to show the gap in awareness."

6. Shaping the Action & Agency (Responsibility)

If your exhibition is incredibly successful, what do you want your audience to do or change?

What is one small, realistic piece of this puzzle we can take action on locally?

We can't solve the whole global problem today, but what can we change in our community?

7. Embedding the Wider Learner Profile

Which additional Learner Profile attributes will be most critical for your group during this exhibition?

Select 2-3 attributes to focus on as a team (in addition to being Reflective and Open-minded).

How will you demonstrate these chosen attributes as you research and plan?

For example: "We will be open-minded when reading about why some communities rely on activities that affect endangered species, even if we disagree."
